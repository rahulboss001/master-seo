01Module

SEO Fundamentals — How Search Engines Work

Before optimising anything, you must deeply understand what you're optimising for. This module covers the engine under the hood.

Beginner Foundation

1.1 What Is SEO & Why It Matters

1.1

SEO (Search Engine Optimisation) is the practice of making your website appear higher in organic (unpaid) search results. It works by aligning your content with what search algorithms reward — relevance, authority, and user experience. Unlike paid ads, SEO traffic compounds over time and doesn't stop when you stop paying. A page that ranks #1 on Google for a keyword gets an average of 27.6% of all clicks for that query. Ranking on page 2 is effectively invisible — only ~0.6% of users scroll to page 2.

📖 Real Example

A local bakery in Delhi invests ₹0 in ads but writes blog posts about "best birthday cake ideas" and "eggless cake recipes". Over 12 months, these pages rank on page 1 of Google. They now receive 800 organic visitors/month who search for exactly what they sell — with zero ongoing ad cost. A competitor pays ₹15,000/month for the same traffic volume via Google Ads.

✅ Your Task
  1. Search your own name or a hobby on Google. Analyse the top 3 results — what do they have in common?
  2. Search a local business in your city. Note: does it show on Google Maps? Does it have a website ranking?
  3. Write a 200-word note answering: "What problem does SEO solve for businesses?"

1.2 Crawling — How Google Discovers Pages

1.2

Googlebot is an automated program that travels the internet like a librarian browsing shelves. It starts from known URLs, visits them, reads the content, and follows every link it finds to discover new pages. This process is called crawling. Crawl budget is the number of pages Google will crawl on your site in a given period — large sites must manage this carefully. Pages Google can't find won't rank, period.

▸
Crawl budget — Google doesn't crawl infinite pages. Small sites get crawled fully; large sites must prioritise important pages.
▸
Internal links — The primary way Googlebot discovers new pages on your site. Orphan pages (no inbound links) may never be found.
▸
robots.txt — A text file at yoursite.com/robots.txt that tells crawlers which pages NOT to crawl. Misconfiguring this can accidentally block your entire site.
📖 Real Example

A new blog post is published on example.com/blog/new-post. Googlebot discovers it only because the blog homepage links to it. If you publish a page with no links pointing to it, it's an "orphan page" — Googlebot may never find it, even if your sitemap lists it.

Check your own site: site:yourdomain.com in Google shows all indexed pages.

✅ Your Task
  1. Visit google.com/robots.txt and nytimes.com/robots.txt — read what's blocked. Note why.
  2. Search site:wikipedia.org in Google. How many pages are indexed? Now try site:yourfavouriteblog.com.
  3. Draw a diagram showing how Googlebot would crawl a 5-page website (Home, About, Blog, Post 1, Contact).
Google Search Console Screaming Frog robots.txt tester

1.3 Indexing — How Google Stores Pages

1.3

After crawling, Google processes the page content and stores it in its index — a massive database of trillions of web pages. Indexed pages are candidates to appear in search results. Not every crawled page gets indexed — Google may decide a page has thin content, is a duplicate of another page, or isn't useful enough. The most common indexing issues are: blocked by robots.txt, noindex tag, duplicate content, thin content, and redirect chains.

📖 Real Example

An e-commerce site has 1,000 product pages. After a developer accidentally adds <meta name="robots" content="noindex"> to a template, 800 product pages get deindexed within 2 weeks. Sales drop 60%. This is one of the most common catastrophic SEO mistakes.

Always check: site:yourdomain.com regularly to monitor indexed page count.

✅ Your Task
  1. Go to Google Search Console (free) and add a website (can be your own or a test site). Check how many pages are indexed vs. not indexed.
  2. Search site:medium.com "your topic" to see how Medium's content ranks. Notice Medium gets many pages indexed — why?
  3. Create a test HTML file with <meta name="robots" content="noindex"> and understand what it does.

1.4 Ranking Algorithms & Key Signals

1.4

Google's ranking algorithm uses 200+ signals to decide which pages deserve to rank on page 1. The three fundamental pillars are: Relevance (does the content match what the user searched for?), Authority (do other trusted sites link to this page?), and Experience (is the page fast, safe, and easy to use?). Google also uses machine learning systems like RankBrain and BERT to better understand natural language queries.

▸
E-E-A-T — Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness. Especially critical for medical, financial, and legal content (YMYL pages).
▸
PageRank — Google's original algorithm. A page gets authority by receiving links from other authoritative pages. Links = votes of trust.
▸
RankBrain — AI that interprets ambiguous queries. "Python" could mean the language or the snake — RankBrain figures out intent from context.
▸
Helpful Content System — Google's 2022–2024 updates reward content written for humans first, penalising AI-mass-produced or thin content.
📖 Real Example

A page about "how to tie a bowline knot" written by a certified sailing instructor with clear step-by-step instructions, diagrams, and 50 inbound links from sailing blogs outranks a generic page with vague text and no links — because it wins on Relevance (complete instructions), Authority (links + author credentials), and Experience (clear, fast, mobile-friendly).

✅ Your Task
  1. Search "best protein powder" and "how to make pasta" — notice how completely different types of results appear. Write down why the results differ.
  2. Open any top-ranking article and use Moz Bar (free extension) to check its Page Authority and Domain Authority.
  3. List 5 signals you think Google uses to rank pages (then look up Google's published ranking factors to compare).

02Module

Keyword Research — The Foundation of SEO Strategy

Keyword research is the process of finding what your target audience searches for. Every SEO decision flows from it. Get this wrong and you'll spend months ranking for terms nobody searches.

Beginner→IntermediateStrategy

2.1 Understanding Search Intent

2.1

Search intent (also called user intent) is the underlying goal of a search query. It's the single most important concept in modern SEO. Google's entire ranking system is built around matching results to intent. Even if your page has the exact keyword and thousands of backlinks, it will NOT rank if it doesn't match intent. There are 4 intent types: Informational (learning something), Navigational (finding a specific site), Commercial (comparing options before buying), Transactional (ready to take action).

▸
Informational: "how to lose weight fast" → user wants a guide/article, not a product page
▸
Navigational: "facebook login" → user wants the Facebook homepage specifically
▸
Commercial: "best laptops under 50000" → user wants comparisons, reviews, lists
▸
Transactional: "buy iPhone 15 online" → user is ready to purchase; product/category page ranks here
📖 Real Example

A gym creates a product page for "protein powder" and stuffs the page with keywords. But the top results for this query are all blog articles (best protein powders list, reviews). The gym's product page will never rank because it doesn't match informational/commercial intent. Solution: create a blog post "10 Best Protein Powders for Beginners in 2024" — then link to your product in the article.

✅ Your Task
  1. Search 10 keywords in your niche. Classify each as Informational / Commercial / Transactional.
  2. For each keyword, look at what TYPE of page ranks #1 (article? product page? homepage?). This tells you intent.
  3. Write 3 keyword ideas for each intent type for a "yoga studio" business.

2.2 Keyword Metrics — Volume, Difficulty, CPC

2.2

When evaluating a keyword, you must analyse three core metrics. Search Volume = how many people search this per month (higher = more potential traffic but also more competition). Keyword Difficulty (KD) = how hard it is to rank on page 1 (0–100 scale, 0 = easy, 100 = nearly impossible without massive authority). CPC (Cost Per Click) = what advertisers pay per click — high CPC means the keyword has commercial value, meaning real money is made from this traffic.

📖 Real Example

Keyword: "credit card" → Volume: 2.2M/month, KD: 96/100, CPC: $7.50 — impossible for a new site.

Keyword: "best credit card for students in India" → Volume: 8,100/month, KD: 32/100, CPC: $3.20 — achievable for a new personal finance blog. This is the type of keyword to target when starting out.

✅ Your Task
  1. Use Ubersuggest (free) or Google Keyword Planner to research 20 keywords in any niche you like.
  2. Build a spreadsheet with columns: Keyword | Volume | KD | CPC | Intent | Priority (High/Med/Low).
  3. Identify 5 "golden" keywords: Volume > 500/month, KD < 40, clear intent.
AhrefsSEMrushUbersuggestGoogle Keyword PlannerKeywords Everywhere

2.3 Long-Tail vs. Short-Tail Keywords

2.3

Short-tail keywords are broad, high-volume terms (1–2 words) like "shoes" or "SEO." They get millions of searches but are dominated by huge brands. Long-tail keywords are specific, multi-word phrases like "best running shoes for flat feet under $100." They have lower volume but dramatically higher conversion rates because the searcher has very specific intent. As a new site or SEO professional, long-tail keywords are your entry point to ranking.

📖 Real Example

Short-tail: "SEO" → 1.5M searches/month. Top results: Wikipedia, Moz, Google's own guide. A new SEO blog has zero chance.

Long-tail: "how to do SEO for a restaurant website" → 1,200 searches/month. KD: 22. A well-written 2,000-word guide from a new blog CAN rank here — and the reader is highly qualified (they run a restaurant).

✅ Your Task
  1. Take any short-tail keyword (e.g., "yoga") and generate 10 long-tail variants using Google's autocomplete — just start typing and note the suggestions.
  2. Check "People Also Ask" for any keyword — these are gold for long-tail content ideas.
  3. Build a "keyword cluster" — group 15 keywords around one main topic (e.g., "home gym setup").

2.4 Competitor Keyword Research

2.4

One of the fastest keyword research strategies is to analyse what keywords your competitors already rank for. This is reverse-engineering success. Tools like Ahrefs and SEMrush let you enter any competitor's domain and see every keyword they rank for, their traffic, and the pages driving it. You can then identify: (1) keywords they rank for that you don't, (2) keywords they rank on page 2 for (easy to outrank with better content), and (3) gaps where you can rank with content they haven't created.

📖 Real Example

You're building an SEO blog. You enter backlinko.com into Ahrefs Site Explorer. You see they rank for "SEO checklist" with 18,000 monthly visits. You analyse their article, notice it's 3 years old and missing newer techniques. You write a more complete, updated version and target the same keyword. Within 6 months you rank #3 and steal significant traffic.

✅ Your Task
  1. Use Ahrefs Free Webmaster Tools or SEMrush's free trial to analyse 2 competitors in your chosen niche.
  2. Find their top 10 traffic-driving pages and list the keywords those pages target.
  3. Identify 3 keywords where their ranking content is outdated or incomplete — these are your content opportunities.

03Module

On-Page SEO — Optimising Every Page

On-page SEO is everything you control directly on a page to help it rank. Mastering this is the single biggest lever for new SEO practitioners.

IntermediateHigh Impact

3.1 Title Tags — Your #1 On-Page Signal

3.1

The title tag is the clickable blue headline that appears in search results. It is the single most important on-page SEO element. It tells Google what your page is about AND convinces users to click. Rules: include your primary keyword (ideally in the first 60 characters), make it compelling and human — not just keyword stuffing, keep it under 60 characters to avoid truncation, and make every title tag on your site unique.

📖 Real Example

Bad title tag:
SEO Tips | Best SEO | SEO Guide 2024 | SEO
(keyword stuffing, unreadable)

Good title tag:
17 Proven SEO Tips to Double Your Traffic in 2024
(keyword "SEO tips" near start, specific number creates curiosity, year signals freshness — 52 chars)

✅ Your Task
  1. Write 5 title tags for the keyword "how to start a blog" — make each one different (question format, number format, how-to format, benefit-led, curiosity gap).
  2. Use Screaming Frog (free up to 500 URLs) to crawl a website and find all missing or duplicate title tags.
  3. Audit 10 title tags on a real website — rewrite any that are weak.

3.2 Meta Descriptions — Improve Your CTR

3.2

The meta description is the 160-character summary below the title in search results. It is NOT a direct ranking factor, but it dramatically affects Click-Through Rate (CTR) — which IS a ranking signal. A compelling meta description convinces users to choose your result over others. Include the keyword (Google bolds it when it matches the search), add a clear benefit or hook, and end with a soft CTA.

📖 Real Example

Weak meta: "This article is about SEO tips. Read our guide to learn SEO."

Strong meta: "Struggling to get traffic? These 17 SEO tips increased our organic visits by 312% in 6 months — all without paid ads. Start with Tip #4."

The strong version creates curiosity, provides proof (312%), sets expectations, and has a hook (Tip #4). It gets 2–3x more clicks.

✅ Your Task
  1. Write meta descriptions for 5 different pages: Homepage, Blog post, Product page, About page, Contact page.
  2. Install SEO Meta in 1 Click (free Chrome extension) and analyse meta descriptions on 10 competitor pages.
  3. Find 3 pages in your niche where meta descriptions are weak or missing — these are pages you can outrank with better CTR.

3.3 Header Tags (H1–H6) & Content Structure

3.3

Header tags (H1, H2, H3, etc.) create a logical document outline that helps Google understand content hierarchy and helps users scan the page. The H1 is the page's main headline — use it once, include your primary keyword. H2s are section headings — include secondary keywords naturally. H3–H6 are subsections. Think of headers like a book's table of contents: they should make the structure obvious at a glance. Google uses headers to understand what sub-topics a page covers.

📖 Real Example

Article: "How to Make Sourdough Bread"
H1: How to Make Sourdough Bread at Home (Beginner's Guide)
H2: What You'll Need (Ingredients & Equipment)
H2: How to Make a Sourdough Starter
H3: Day 1: Starting the Fermentation
H3: Day 3–7: Feeding Your Starter
H2: Mixing and Shaping Your Dough
H2: Baking: Time & Temperature Guide
This structure helps Google know the article covers the full topic — starter, mixing, baking — increasing chances of ranking for all related searches.

✅ Your Task
  1. Pick any blog article online. Map out its header structure (H1, H2, H3). Does it make logical sense?
  2. Plan the full header structure for an article titled "Complete Guide to Home Workout for Beginners" — write all H1, H2, H3 headers before writing a single word of content.
  3. Use Screaming Frog to check a site for missing H1s or pages with multiple H1s.

3.4 URL Structure & Slug Optimisation

3.4

The URL slug is the part after the domain (e.g., yoursite.com/seo-tips-for-beginners). Good URLs are short, descriptive, use hyphens (not underscores), contain the primary keyword, and avoid stop words (a, the, for, of). Bad URLs like yoursite.com/?p=12344 or yoursite.com/page1/category3/post-from-2018-march-12-seo tell Google and users nothing. URL structure also affects internal linking and site architecture.

📖 Real Example

Bad URLs:
yoursite.com/2024/03/14/?p=9823
yoursite.com/category/subcategory/how-to-do-search-engine-optimization-tips-and-tricks-for-beginners-in-2024

Good URL:
yoursite.com/seo-tips
yoursite.com/blog/keyword-research-guide

Shorter, cleaner URLs get more clicks and are easier to share.

✅ Your Task
  1. Rewrite these bad URLs into good ones: /blog/post-123, /the-complete-and-ultimate-guide-to-seo-for-absolute-beginners-2024, /p?id=4421&cat=12.
  2. Audit the URL structure of any website using Screaming Frog — find 5 URLs that need improvement.
  3. Set up a WordPress or any CMS blog and configure the permalink structure to /post-name/.

3.5 Image Optimisation

3.5

Images affect SEO in three ways: (1) Page speed — unoptimised images are the #1 cause of slow pages, which hurts rankings. (2) Alt text — the text description of an image that Google reads, since it can't "see" images the way humans do. (3) Image search traffic — properly tagged images can rank in Google Images and drive traffic. Best practices: compress images (use WebP format), write descriptive alt text with keywords, use descriptive filenames (seo-tips-checklist.jpg not IMG_4421.jpg), and serve images at correct dimensions.

📖 Real Example

Bad image alt text: alt="" or alt="image1"

Good image alt text: alt="SEO checklist showing 17 on-page optimisation steps"

A recipe blog used descriptive alt text on all food images. 18 months later, 22% of their total traffic came from Google Images alone — a channel they hadn't even intentionally optimised for. Image filename was also changed from IMG_9234.jpg to chocolate-fudge-brownie-recipe.jpg.

✅ Your Task
  1. Take any 10-image webpage and write proper alt text for every image.
  2. Use TinyPNG or Squoosh.app to compress 5 images — note the file size reduction (aim for under 150KB per image).
  3. Use Screaming Frog to find all images missing alt text on a website.
TinyPNGSquooshShortPixelWebP Converter

3.6 Internal Linking Strategy

3.6

Internal links are links from one page on your site to another page on your site. They serve two critical purposes: (1) They help Googlebot discover and understand the relationships between your pages. (2) They distribute PageRank (link equity) across your site — linking from a strong page to a weaker page boosts the weaker page. The anchor text (the clickable text of the link) also signals to Google what the destination page is about. A well-structured internal linking strategy can significantly improve rankings without any external link building.

📖 Real Example

A website had a "SEO Tools Comparison" page stuck on page 3 of Google. The SEO team found that their most powerful page (Domain Authority 60, with 200 backlinks) — their "SEO Guide" homepage — had zero internal links to the Tools Comparison page. They added 3 contextual internal links from the guide. Within 8 weeks, the Tools Comparison page moved from position 28 to position 7 — no new backlinks, just internal linking.

✅ Your Task
  1. Take any 5-page website and draw an internal linking map — which pages link to which?
  2. Identify 3 "orphan pages" (pages with no internal links pointing to them) using Screaming Frog.
  3. For a blog with 10 articles, create an internal linking plan showing which articles should link to each other and why.
04Module

Technical SEO — The Foundation Beneath the Surface

Technical SEO ensures that search engines can crawl, index, and understand your site without obstacles. Even perfect content fails to rank if technical issues block it.

AdvancedHigh Impact

4.1 Site Speed & Core Web Vitals

4.1

Core Web Vitals are Google's official page experience metrics — they directly affect rankings. The three main metrics are: LCP (Largest Contentful Paint) — how fast the main content loads (target: under 2.5 seconds). INP (Interaction to Next Paint) — how fast the page responds to interactions (target: under 200ms). CLS (Cumulative Layout Shift) — how much the layout jumps around as it loads (target: under 0.1). These are measured as real user data via Chrome browser reports.

📖 Real Example

An e-commerce site had an LCP of 8.2 seconds (poor) due to an unoptimised hero image (4MB JPEG). After converting the image to WebP and using lazy loading, LCP dropped to 1.8 seconds. Within 3 months, the homepage moved from position 14 to position 4 for their main commercial keyword — generating an additional $12,000/month in revenue from organic traffic alone.

✅ Your Task
  1. Run any website through PageSpeed Insights (pagespeed.web.dev) — note the LCP, INP, CLS scores.
  2. Run 5 competitor sites and compare their Core Web Vitals scores — does faster = higher ranking in your niche?
  3. List 5 specific actions that would improve LCP for a slow website based on the PSI report.
PageSpeed InsightsGTmetrixChrome DevToolsWebPageTest

4.2 XML Sitemaps

4.2

An XML sitemap is a file (yoursite.com/sitemap.xml) that lists all the important URLs on your website — like a roadmap given directly to search engines. It helps Google discover pages faster, especially new or deep pages that might not have many inbound links. A sitemap should include: all important pages, their last-modified date, and their change frequency. Crucially, it should NOT include pages you don't want indexed (404s, duplicate pages, thin content pages).

📖 Real Example

A news website published 50 articles/day. Without a news sitemap, Google took 2–3 days to discover new articles. After implementing a Google News XML sitemap, new articles appeared in search results within 10–15 minutes — critical for news SEO where freshness determines ranking. Visit nytimes.com/sitemap.xml to see a real-world example.

✅ Your Task
  1. Visit amazon.com/sitemap.xml and any small blog's sitemap. Compare the structure — what's different?
  2. Create a sitemap for a 10-page fictional website using a free tool (XML-sitemaps.com).
  3. Submit a sitemap via Google Search Console and check for any reported errors in the Coverage report.

4.3 HTTPS & Site Security

4.3

HTTPS (HyperText Transfer Protocol Secure) encrypts the connection between a user's browser and your server. Google confirmed HTTPS as a ranking signal in 2014. Beyond rankings, sites without HTTPS display a "Not Secure" warning in Chrome — this massively reduces trust and conversion rates. An SSL certificate (what enables HTTPS) is free via Let's Encrypt. All new sites must launch with HTTPS. If migrating from HTTP to HTTPS, every URL must 301 redirect to its HTTPS equivalent.

📖 Real Example

A freelance photographer's portfolio was at http://sarahphotos.com. When Chrome started showing "Not Secure" in the address bar, her contact form submissions dropped 40% — clients were scared off by the warning before filling in their details. After migrating to HTTPS with proper 301 redirects, form conversions returned to normal and her rankings for "wedding photographer Delhi" improved by 6 positions.

✅ Your Task
  1. Check any 10 websites — how many are HTTP vs HTTPS? Note if HTTP sites get the "Not Secure" warning.
  2. If you have a website, migrate it to HTTPS using your hosting provider's free SSL (most offer Let's Encrypt).
  3. Use Why No Padlock (whynopadlock.com) to check if any resources on an HTTPS site are still loading over HTTP (mixed content).

4.4 Canonical Tags & Duplicate Content

4.4

Duplicate content is when identical or very similar content exists on multiple URLs. This confuses Google about which page to rank — and it may rank none of them well, splitting authority across duplicates. The canonical tag <link rel="canonical" href="preferred-URL"> tells Google which version is the "original" when multiple URLs show similar content. Common duplicate content scenarios: HTTP vs HTTPS, www vs non-www, trailing slash vs no trailing slash, faceted navigation (e.g., /products?color=red&size=M).

📖 Real Example

An e-commerce site had 1 product but it was accessible via 4 URLs:
http://site.com/product
https://site.com/product
https://www.site.com/product
https://site.com/product?ref=homepage
Google was splitting authority across 4 "different" pages. Adding a canonical tag pointing all variants to https://www.site.com/product consolidated the authority, and the product jumped from position 18 to position 5.

✅ Your Task
  1. Check if a website has duplicate content by searching: site:example.com "exact phrase from their homepage" — do multiple pages appear?
  2. Use Screaming Frog to crawl a website and check the Canonical tab — are canonicals set correctly?
  3. Write the correct canonical tag HTML for a product page that exists at both /product/shoes and /sale/shoes.

4.5 Structured Data & Schema Markup

4.5

Structured data is code added to your pages (in JSON-LD format) that explicitly tells Google what your content is about. It enables rich results — enhanced search listings with stars, prices, images, FAQs, cooking times, etc. — that significantly increase CTR. Common schema types include: Article, Product, Recipe, FAQ, Review, LocalBusiness, Event, HowTo. Implementing schema doesn't guarantee rich results, but it's a strong signal and a major competitive advantage when competitors don't use it.

📖 Real Example

A recipe blog added FAQ schema to their bread recipes. The result: their search listing expanded to show 4 collapsible Q&As directly in search results, taking up 3x the space. Their CTR jumped from 4.2% to 11.8% — nearly tripling their click volume with zero change in ranking position. Schema markup is one of the highest-ROI technical SEO activities available.

✅ Your Task
  1. Use Google's Rich Results Test (search.google.com/test/rich-results) to test any page — does it have valid schema?
  2. Use Schema.org's markup generator or Google's Structured Data Markup Helper to create FAQ schema for a 5-question FAQ page.
  3. Add Product schema to a mock product page with: name, price, rating, availability, description.

05Module

Content Strategy & Writing for SEO

Content is the fuel of SEO. But not all content ranks. Learn to create content that satisfies both search algorithms and real human readers.

IntermediateCore Skill

5.1 Topic Clusters & Pillar Pages

5.1

A topic cluster is a content strategy where one comprehensive "pillar page" covers a broad topic, and multiple "cluster" pages cover sub-topics — all interlinked. This structure signals to Google that your site has deep topical authority on a subject. Instead of having 30 unrelated blog posts, you have 5 clusters of 6 posts each — each cluster establishing your site as the authority on that specific topic. E.g., a fitness site: Pillar = "Complete Guide to Weight Training" + Clusters = "beginner exercises," "workout schedule," "nutrition for muscle gain," "recovery tips."

📖 Real Example

HubSpot pioneered topic clusters in 2017. They reorganised their blog from hundreds of isolated posts into topic clusters around "inbound marketing," "CRM," "social media," etc. Within 8 months, their domain authority increased significantly and organic traffic doubled. Google rewarded their topical depth — their pillar pages now dominate page 1 for major marketing terms.

✅ Your Task
  1. Choose a niche (e.g., "personal finance for students"). Create a topic cluster map with 1 pillar page and 8 cluster articles.
  2. Identify the primary keyword for the pillar page and secondary keywords for each cluster article.
  3. Plan the internal linking structure — which cluster articles link to the pillar, and which link to each other?

5.2 Writing SEO Content — The AIDA + SEO Framework

5.2

Great SEO content achieves two things simultaneously: it satisfies search intent AND keeps readers engaged. The structure: Attention (hook in first 100 words — tell them what they'll get), Interest (sub-topics that prove depth and breadth), Desire (examples, proof, specifics that build trust), Action (CTA at the end). Combine this with SEO: use your primary keyword in first 100 words, use LSI (related) keywords naturally throughout, answer the user's question completely, and use short paragraphs + headers for scannability.

📖 Real Example

Weak opening: "SEO is important. In this article we will discuss SEO tips."

Strong SEO opening: "If your website gets fewer than 100 visitors a month, there's likely one root cause — and it has nothing to do with your design, your niche, or how long you've been publishing. In this guide, I'll show you 12 SEO mistakes most beginners make (and the exact fixes that helped me grow to 50,000 monthly visitors in 14 months)."

The second version hooks with a specific problem, promises a specific solution, and builds immediate trust.

✅ Your Task
  1. Write the opening 200 words for an article titled "How to Learn Python Programming for Beginners" — make it impossible to stop reading.
  2. Take any weak article online and rewrite just the first paragraph to be more compelling.
  3. Write a full 800-word SEO article on a topic you know well — optimise for a specific keyword and check keyword density using Yoast or Rank Math.
Surfer SEOClearscopeYoast SEORank MathHemingway App

5.3 Content Freshness & Updating Old Content

5.3

Google's "Query Deserves Freshness" (QDF) algorithm boosts fresh content for queries where recency matters (news, annual updates, evolving topics). But freshness isn't just about new content — regularly updating old content can produce faster ranking gains than creating new content from scratch. When you update a post: refresh statistics to the current year, add new sections covering recent developments, improve existing sections, update the published date, and add new internal links. This signals to Google the page is current and comprehensive.

📖 Real Example

Backlinko's "SEO Techniques" article was written in 2018 and ranked #4. By 2021 it had slipped to #11 as newer content appeared. Brian Dean updated the article: added 3,000 new words covering 2021 tactics, updated all screenshots, refreshed statistics, changed "2018" to "2021" in the title. Within 6 weeks it jumped back to #2. The update took 4 hours — far faster than writing a new post that would take months to gain authority.

✅ Your Task
  1. Find a blog post from 2020 or earlier in any niche that still ranks on page 2. List 5 specific ways you would update it to make it rank on page 1.
  2. Set up a content audit spreadsheet with columns: URL, Publish Date, Traffic (from GSC), Last Updated, Action Needed.
  3. Update one of your own blog posts (or a test post) and submit it for re-indexing via Google Search Console.
06Module

Link Building & Off-Page SEO

Backlinks remain the strongest ranking signal after content. Building high-quality links is an art form that separates good SEOs from great ones.

AdvancedHigh Authority

6.1 What Makes a Backlink Valuable

6.1

Not all backlinks are equal. A single link from The New York Times is worth more than 10,000 links from random low-quality blogs. Backlink value is determined by: Domain Authority (the linking site's overall trust level), Relevance (is the linking site in your industry?), Link placement (editorial links in body text are more valuable than footer/sidebar links), Anchor text (keyword-rich anchor is more powerful but must be natural), and DoFollow vs NoFollow (DoFollow passes link equity; NoFollow signals no endorsement but still has some value).

📖 Real Example

Weak backlink: A link from a directory site like "toplinksdirectory.xyz" with Domain Rating 3, no real traffic, no editorial standards — may actually HURT your rankings (Google's Penguin algorithm penalises manipulative links).

Strong backlink: A mention in a Forbes article "Best SEO Tools for Small Business" with an editorial link in the body of the article from a site with Domain Rating 94 — this single link can propel a page from position 15 to position 3.

✅ Your Task
  1. Use Ahrefs Free Backlink Checker to analyse the backlink profiles of 3 websites in your niche. Note their top linking domains.
  2. Evaluate 10 backlinks from a site — classify each as High/Medium/Low quality and explain why.
  3. Create a "link prospecting list" — 20 websites in your niche that would make ideal link sources.

6.2 Guest Posting

6.2

Guest posting means writing an article for another website and receiving a link back to yours in return. It remains one of the most effective and Google-approved link building tactics when done legitimately — writing genuinely useful content for reputable sites. The process: find relevant sites that accept guest posts, analyse their content quality and domain authority, pitch a specific article idea (not a generic "I want to guest post" email), write an exceptional article, include 1–2 natural links back to relevant content on your site.

📖 Real Example

An SEO consultant pitched 12 marketing blogs with a specific article: "Why Your SEO Strategy Is Missing This One Step (With Data from 47 Campaigns)." 4 said yes — including Search Engine Journal (DR 89) and Search Engine Land (DR 92). The 4 guest posts generated 6 backlinks, drove 3,400 referral visitors, and contributed to their domain authority jumping from 34 to 47 over 6 months. They also got 2 direct client inquiries from readers.

✅ Your Task
  1. Find 10 websites in your niche that accept guest posts. Use: your niche + "write for us" in Google.
  2. Write a guest post pitch email for one of those sites — include a specific article idea, why it's relevant to their audience, and your credentials.
  3. Write a full 1,000-word sample guest post on any topic — this becomes part of your SEO portfolio.

6.3 Broken Link Building

6.3

Broken link building is a link building tactic where you find pages that have links pointing to dead/404 pages, create content that replaces the dead resource, and notify the linking site so they can update their link to point to your working content instead. It's effective because you're helping the website owner fix a real problem (a broken link damages their user experience). They have every incentive to update the link — and you get a valuable backlink.

📖 Real Example

An SEO agency found a popular "Resources for Small Business Owners" page on a Chamber of Commerce website (DR 71) that linked to a government small business PDF that had been moved and was now a 404. They created a comprehensive "Small Business SEO Starter Kit" PDF, emailed the Chamber: "Hey, your resources page has a broken link to [URL]. We've created an updated version at [our URL] — might be useful as a replacement." The Chamber updated the link within 48 hours. One email = one high-quality government-adjacent backlink.

✅ Your Task
  1. Use Check My Links (Chrome extension) to scan any resource page in your niche for broken links.
  2. Find 5 broken link opportunities and write the replacement content idea for each.
  3. Write a broken link building outreach email template — personable, helpful, not pushy.

6.4 Digital PR & Linkable Assets

6.4

Digital PR is earning backlinks by creating content so valuable, original, or newsworthy that other sites naturally link to it. Linkable assets include: original research/surveys with unique data, comprehensive guides that become industry references, free tools and calculators, infographics with compelling statistics, and controversial opinion pieces that generate debate. When done well, one linkable asset can earn 50–500 backlinks passively over years — making it one of the most scalable link building strategies.

Backlinko published "We Analyzed 11.8 Million Google Search Results" — an original research study on ranking factors. This single piece earned 5,600+ backlinks from sites like Forbes, TechCrunch, and Search Engine Land. The cost? 2 weeks of data analysis. But it continues to earn links 5 years later. This is the power of linkable assets — create once, earn links indefinitely.

7.1 Google Business Profile Optimisation

7.1

Google Business Profile (formerly Google My Business) is the single most important asset for local SEO. It's what powers the Google Maps 3-pack — the 3 local business results shown above organic results for local searches. A fully optimised GBP includes: accurate name/address/phone (NAP), correct business category, complete business description with keywords, all opening hours, photos (regularly updated), services/products listed, Q&A section answered, regular Google Posts, and active review management.

7.2 Local Citations & NAP Consistency

7.2

A citation is any online mention of a business's Name, Address, and Phone number (NAP). Citations appear on directories like Justdial, Sulekha, IndiaMart, Yelp, Yellow Pages, TripAdvisor, and hundreds of industry-specific directories. Google cross-references citations to verify a business is real and trustworthy. NAP inconsistency (e.g., "123 Main St." on one site and "123 Main Street" on another) confuses Google and weakens local rankings. Building 50–100 consistent citations is a foundational local SEO task.

📖 Real Example

A restaurant in Bengaluru was listed as "Spice Garden Restaurant" on their website, "Spice Garden" on Zomato, "Spice Garden Res." on JustDial, and "The Spice Garden" on Google Maps. This inconsistency meant Google couldn't confidently verify the business's details. After standardising NAP across 70 directories to exactly "Spice Garden Restaurant" and ensuring address format matched perfectly, local rankings for "Indian restaurant Koramangala" improved from position 11 to position 3 in Maps within 90 days.

✅ Your Task

  2. Build a list of the top 30 citation sources relevant to your country/city (Google Maps, JustDial, Sulekha, Yellow Pages India, etc.).
  3. Submit a fictional business to 5 citation sources — practice the process end-to-end.

7.3 Review Management & Reputation SEO

7.3

Reviews are one of the top 3 local ranking factors in Google Maps. More importantly, they directly influence whether people choose your client's business. Strategy: (1) Systemise review generation — ask every customer via text/email with a direct review link. (2) Respond to every review (positive and negative) — response rate is a local ranking signal. (3) Never fake reviews — Google's algorithms detect them and can suspend the GBP entirely. (4) Address negative reviews professionally — 45% of consumers say a business responding to negative reviews makes them more likely to visit.

8.1 Category Page Optimisation

8.1

Category pages are the highest-value pages on most e-commerce sites — they rank for high-volume commercial keywords ("running shoes," "laptop bags") and funnel users to products. Yet most e-commerce sites treat category pages as mere product lists. Optimised category pages include: an SEO-friendly H1 with the main keyword, a 200–300 word editorial introduction above or below products (describing the category, materials, use cases), breadcrumb navigation, internally linked sub-categories, user-generated content (reviews), and proper canonical tags for filtered versions.

📖 Real Example

Zappos.com's "Women's Running Shoes" category page ranks #2 for that keyword (720,000 monthly searches) because it has: a keyword-rich H1, a 300-word introduction explaining what to look for in running shoes, structured filters with proper canonical handling, 1,200+ product listings, 45,000+ customer reviews, and breadcrumb schema. It's not just a product grid — it's a content-rich, authoritative page that answers "which running shoes should I buy?"

✅ Your Task
  1. Analyse 3 e-commerce category pages — list every SEO element present and missing from each.
  2. Write a 300-word optimised category description for "Wireless Headphones" — include target keyword naturally 3–4 times.
  3. Create a category page SEO checklist with 15 items.

8.2 Product Page SEO

8.2

Product pages must rank for specific product-intent keywords AND convert visitors. Key optimisation points: unique product descriptions (never copy manufacturer descriptions — Google treats them as thin/duplicate content), keyword-optimised title tags (Brand + Model + Key Feature + Product Type), product schema markup for rich results (price, rating, availability), multiple high-quality images with keyword-optimised alt text, user-generated reviews (both for trust and keyword diversity), clear CTA buttons, and breadcrumb navigation.

📖 Real Example

Bad product title tag: "Sony WH-1000XM5"
Good product title tag: "Sony WH-1000XM5 Wireless Noise Cancelling Headphones — 30Hr Battery | Sony India"

The good title includes the model name (navigational), product category keywords (wireless headphones, noise cancelling), a key feature (30hr battery), and brand/regional identifier. It targets multiple search queries simultaneously while remaining under 70 characters.

✅ Your Task

  3. Find 5 products on any e-commerce site using manufacturer descriptions — identify how you'd make each unique.

10.1 Featured Snippets — Position Zero

10.1

Featured snippets are the answer boxes that appear above all organic results (Position 0). They get ~8% of clicks on average but appear for 12% of all searches. To win a featured snippet: your page must already rank in the top 10, the query must be a question or comparison, your content must have a clear, direct answer. Format the answer in the format Google tends to use for that query type: paragraph (questions), numbered list (how-to steps), table (comparisons), bulleted list (features/items). Put the direct answer in a clear paragraph immediately after the question as a subheading.

📖 Real Example

A page targeting "how long does it take to learn SEO" was ranking #4. To capture the featured snippet, the SEO rewrote the opening section:

H2: How Long Does It Take to Learn SEO?
"Learning basic SEO takes 2–4 weeks. Becoming proficient enough to drive real results takes 3–6 months of practice. Reaching expert level — where you can lead SEO strategy for large sites — takes 1–3 years of hands-on experience."

This direct, concise, structured answer captured the featured snippet within 3 weeks, increasing CTR from 4.2% to 12.1%.

✅ Your Task
  1. Find 10 "question" keywords in your niche. Search each and note which ones have featured snippets and what format they use.
  2. Take a page ranking #4–10 for a question keyword and rewrite a section to target the featured snippet.
  3. Identify 5 featured snippet opportunities where you could rank if you published the right content.

10.2 SEO for AI Overviews (GEO)

10.2

Google's AI Overviews (formerly SGE) now appear for many queries, summarising answers from multiple sources. This has changed SEO: AI Overviews can reduce clicks even when you rank #1. The new goal is to be cited IN the AI Overview — this is called Generative Engine Optimisation (GEO). To get cited: produce content with original data, cite reputable sources, use clear structured writing that's easy to extract, build strong E-E-A-T signals, use FAQ sections, and target informational queries with direct answers. Sites with strong E-E-A-T and structured content are cited most frequently.

📖 Real Example

A health website noticed organic traffic dropped 18% after Google started showing AI Overviews for their main keywords. But they also noticed their brand appeared as a cited source in 34 different AI Overviews, driving "brand search" traffic up 41%. The shift: instead of competing for clicks, they optimised to be THE authoritative source cited — every article ends with a clear summary box, all statistics are sourced, and each article has an author bio with credentials. This is the new frontier of SEO.

✅ Your Task
  1. Search 20 queries in your niche — note which ones trigger AI Overviews. Which sources are cited?
  2. Rewrite one blog article to include: a clear summary box, numbered key takeaways, and cited statistics — optimising for AI citation.
  3. Research and write a report on how AI Overviews are changing SEO strategy — what should SEOs focus on in 2025?

Audit process: (1) Technical crawl with Screaming Frog, (2) Google Search Console analysis, (3) GA4 traffic analysis, (4) Backlink profile analysis, (5) Keyword ranking analysis, (6) Competitor gap analysis, (7) Content quality review, (8) Prioritised action list by impact and effort.
